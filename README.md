## Abstract

Advances in large language models (LLMs) have driven a rapid shift in the field of natural language processing. The integration of LLMs across applications such as question answering, summarization, and dialogue generation has significantly improved the generation of coherent and contextually appropriate text. However, despite their strong predictive performance, LLMs remain susceptible to *hallucinations* — outputs that appear realistic but are factually incorrect or logically inconsistent, lacking sufficient evidential grounding.

The causes of hallucinations span multiple areas: limitations and biases in training data, architectural design choices, optimization objectives, and inference-time factors such as prompting and decoding strategies. This paper provides a clear and structured overview of hallucinations in generative LLMs and offers guidelines for correctly identifying each level of hallucination within a coherent taxonomy.

We also examine how hallucinations manifest across the LLM lifecycle and in real-world applications where accuracy is critical, including healthcare, education, media, and legal contexts. We further review the most common mechanisms and techniques used to detect and mitigate hallucinations in LLM outputs. Finally, we position our analysis within the existing survey literature and highlight open directions for improving the reliability and trustworthiness of LLMs as adoption of these technologies accelerates.

**Index Terms:** Large Language Models, Hallucination, Detection, Mitigation, Reliability

## Key Contributions

- A structured taxonomy of hallucinations in generative LLMs
- An analysis of how hallucinations emerge across the LLM lifecycle
- A review of detection and mitigation techniques
- A discussion of hallucination risks in high-stakes domains (healthcare, education, media, legal)
- Identification of open research directions for improving LLM reliability
